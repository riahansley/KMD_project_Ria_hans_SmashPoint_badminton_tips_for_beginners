History

History of Badminton

The sport of badminton, as recognized today, developed during the mid-19th century, evolving from earlier recreational games involving a shuttlecock and racquets. While the precise origins of the game are uncertain, it is known that British expatriates in India formalized rules and competitive play, transforming a casual pastime into an organized sport.

The name “badminton” is associated with Badminton House, the estate of the Duke of Beaufort in Gloucestershire, though the connection between the location and the game’s adoption remains unclear. Historical records indicate that early attempts to document the game occurred in the 1860s, though few of these sources have survived. Contemporary accounts describe a version of the game played with opposing teams, separated by a string or net, reflecting the foundational structure of modern badminton.

Badminton in the Early Years

In its earliest form, badminton emerged among British residents in India, where it was commonly called Poona or Poonah after the city where it gained widespread attention. Originally, the game did not have a fixed number of players, but over time it was formalized for either singles or doubles matches. The shuttlecocks used during this period were typically reinforced with India rubber to improve durability and flight.

Modern Badminton Rules

Badminton continued to be played according to the Pune rules until 1887, when J. H. E. Hart of the Bath Badminton Club introduced a set of revised regulations, laying the foundation for a more standardized version of the game.

These regulations were subsequently published by the Badminton Association of England (BAE) in 1893, marking the official establishment of the sport in Portsmouth. The first organized tournaments took place in 1899, with singles competitions being incorporated the following year, in 1900.
